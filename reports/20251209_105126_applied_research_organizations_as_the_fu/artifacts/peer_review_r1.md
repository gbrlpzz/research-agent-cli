## 1. Overall Assessment
**[X] Minor Revisions**
The document presents a compelling, theoretically grounded argument regarding the ascent of RTOs and FROs. The integration of Marblestone’s work on FROs with Mazzucato’s Mission-Oriented framework is a highlight. However, the manuscript requires revisions to address the specific sub-questions regarding University Technology Transfer Offices (TTOs) and to nuance the claim of "displacement" versus "complementation."

## 2. Strengths
*   **Theoretical Synthesis:** Successfully bridges the gap between traditional Innovation Systems literature (RTOs/NIS) and emerging institutional design theories (FROs/Marblestone). The connection between "Pasteur's Quadrant" and the FRO operational model is well-articulated.
*   **Evidence-Based Reasoning:** The use of Comin et al. (`@Do_Companies_Be_Comin_2019`) provides necessary empirical grounding for the claims regarding RTO impact on private firm performance, moving the paper beyond purely theoretical assertion.
*   **Clarity of "Valley of Death":** The document defines the TRL gaps clearly and uses this framework effectively to distinguish between University, RTO, and Corporate roles.

## 3. Critical Issues
*   **Gap in Research Plan (TTOs):** One of the specific sub-questions in the Research Plan asks for a comparison to "standard university technology transfer offices." The current draft critiques university *labs* (incentives for publication), but it fails to address the specific failures or limitations of the **TTO model** (e.g., licensing friction, IP valuation disputes). The distinction between a university lab and a university TTO is significant and currently missing.
*   **Overstatement of "Displacement":** The Introduction claims AROs are "displacing" universities. However, the body of the text (specifically the RTO Advantage section) describes them as "critical complements" or bridging institutions. "Displacement" implies universities are losing relevance, whereas the evidence suggests a division of labor. The thesis should be softened to reflect *specialization* rather than *displacement*, or more evidence is needed to prove universities are actually retreating from the innovation landscape.
*   **Missing "Corporate Lab" Analysis:** The Research Plan asks how AROs differ from "siloed corporate R&D labs." While the text mentions corporate labs are risk-averse, it does not analyze *why* the ARO governance model (e.g., non-profit status, longer time horizons) is superior specifically to the corporate lab model. It mostly compares AROs to Universities.

## 4. Suggestions
*   **Nuance the "Linear Model" Critique:** While the linear model is outdated, ensure the text acknowledges that AROs still depend on the basic science output of universities. The ecosystem is cyclical, not just a pipeline that bypasses academia.
*   **Expand on Deep Tech:** The Research Plan mentions "global trends in deep tech." The current draft mentions this briefly in the Conclusion. Expanding on how FROs specifically cater to "Deep Tech" (high technical risk, long timeline) in the "Emerging Institutional Models" section would strengthen the answer to the third sub-question.

## 5. Missing Content
*   **Intellectual Property (IP) Frameworks:** To adequately answer the question regarding comparisons to TTOs, the author should briefly mention how RTOs handle IP (often retaining it or co-owning it to license broadly) versus the TTO model (often exclusive licensing to a single spinoff).
*   **The Decline of Corporate Labs:** To support the comparison to corporate R&D, a sentence or citation regarding the decline of institutions like Bell Labs or Xerox PARC would contextualize *why* RTOs/FROs are filling this specific void.

(Unless the author has specific data showing a reduction in university influence, "displacing" is too strong).
